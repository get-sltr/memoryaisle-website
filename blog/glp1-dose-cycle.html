Eat with your dose, not against it.

Your appetite isn't flat across the week on a GLP-1. Here's the 7-day arc most people are fighting — and how to plan around it.

6 min read · Body composition

Most people on a weekly GLP-1 think of the medication as a thing they take once and then forget about. The shot goes in Sunday morning. The week goes on.

What's actually happening is more interesting. Semaglutide (Wegovy, Ozempic) has a half-life of about 7 days. Tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) sits around 5. After your shot the drug rises to a peak somewhere between 24 and 72 hours, then declines steadily until the next dose. Your appetite tracks that curve. The hardest day to eat is rarely shot day itself — it's the day after. The easiest is the day before your next one.

Shot day Peak (day 1–3) Trough (day 6–7)

Once you see the shape of the curve, the eating strategy reorganizes itself. You stop trying to eat the same way every day and start matching what your body can handle. Same week, same target, completely different plates.

I · The Peak (days 1–3).

This is when food is hardest. Nausea risk is highest. Your stomach feels full after a few bites. Smells are heightened, often unpleasantly — cooking meat or warm rice can be enough to kill the meal.

Strategy here isn't variety. It's protein density per bite. Whatever you can keep down should be carrying as much protein as possible. This is the time for cottage cheese, Greek yogurt, protein shakes, eggs, simple grilled chicken. Skip the heavy carbs, the rich sauces, the high-fat foods that slow gastric emptying further. Drink electrolytes between meals because you'll naturally drink less.

Aim for 3 small protein hits, 25 to 35 grams each. If you only finish two, you still cleared 50 grams. That's a survivable peak.

II · The Window (days 4–5).

This is the sweet spot most people don't recognize. The drug is still active enough to keep you in a calorie deficit without effort, but the side effects have eased. Food tastes like food again. You can finish a normal-sized meal without feeling sick.

Use this window. This is when you cook the better meals — the salmon with the actual side dishes, the steak with the salad, the meal-prep batch that gets you through the next peak. This is also when you can train hard and your body will use the protein you eat to rebuild.

The mistake most people make in the window is undershooting. They've been eating tiny meals for three days; they keep eating tiny meals out of habit. Your appetite has come back partway. Lean into it. Hit your protein target plus a comfortable buffer of complex carbs and healthy fats.

III · The Trough (days 6–7).

By day six the drug has decayed enough that you'll often feel close to your old appetite. Hunger comes back. Cravings come back. The temptation to "make up" for the peak with a binge is real and unhelpful.

Two things matter in the trough. First, don't undo the deficit. The week's calorie average is what determines your weight loss. A 4,000-calorie binge on day six erases the deficit you built across days one through five. Second, this is the perfect day to train. Your appetite supports the recovery food. Your body composition rewards the muscular stimulus.

Eat normally. Slightly above maintenance is fine if you trained. Hit protein. Sleep well. The new dose is tomorrow.

What about Rybelsus and the orals?

Daily oral GLP-1 medications — Rybelsus and now Wegovy Pill — have a much shorter half-life. Instead of one big peak per week, you get a smaller daily one. There's still a cycle; it just runs in 24-hour pulses rather than 7-day ones.

The eating principles are the same. Plan your harder meals for later in the day, after the morning peak has worn off. Front-load protein at every meal, regardless of whether you "feel" the appetite suppression that day. Distribute your intake. Train when food feels easy.

The lesson.

Most people on a GLP-1 are fighting the cycle — eating like they did before the medication on the days they can, starving on the days they can't, and ending the week with a calorie deficit but a protein gap. That's how you lose weight and lose muscle.

Plan with the curve. Heavy protein on the peak. Real meals in the window. Training and recovery in the trough. The medication does the appetite work. Your job is to put the right food in front of the smaller appetite it gave you.
