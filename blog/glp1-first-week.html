Your first week on a GLP-1: eat less, keep your muscle.

Your appetite will handle the “less” on its own. Your job is to make the little you eat count — so you lose fat and keep the muscle that keeps your metabolism strong.

6 min read · Getting started

The first week on a GLP-1 medication is strange in a way nobody quite warns you about. You are not white-knuckling cravings. The cravings just go quiet. The food that ran your life last month suddenly feels like a chore, and a few bites in you are already full. It can feel like a relief and a little disorienting at the same time.

Here is the reframe that makes week one click: your job is not to eat as little as possible. Your appetite is going to handle the “less” part on its own. Your job is to make the small amount you do eat count, so you lose fat and keep the muscle that keeps your metabolism strong. That single shift, from “eat less” to “eat smart on less,” is what this guide is about.

What the first week actually feels like

Most people notice appetite drop within a day or two of the first dose. Food gets less interesting, portions that used to feel normal now feel like too much, and some people get a wave of mild nausea, usually worst around days two through four and easing by the end of the week. None of this means something is wrong. It is the medication doing exactly what it is designed to do, slowing how fast your stomach empties and turning down hunger signals.

The discomfort, when it shows up, is usually mild and short. The way you eat has a big effect on how mild it stays. So week one is less about a strict plan and more about a few gentle habits.

The one thing that matters most: protein

When your total food intake drops fast, your body looks for energy wherever it can find it, and muscle is an easy target. Losing muscle is exactly what you do not want, because muscle is what keeps you strong, keeps your metabolism up, and is the difference between losing fat and just losing weight.

So protein is the non-negotiable. Make it the first thing on your plate at every meal, before the carbs or the sides, because you may get full before you finish. Many clinicians suggest landing protein at each meal, often in the range of 20 to 30 grams, and treating it as the anchor of the day. If you only get one nutrition thing right this week, get this one right.

Your appetite handles the “less.” You handle the “make it count.”

What to eat in week one

Lean toward foods that are gentle on the stomach and protein-forward. Good week-one options include:

  • Eggs, scrambled soft or as an omelet
  • Plain or lightly sweetened Greek yogurt and cottage cheese
  • Grilled or poached chicken, turkey, and white fish
  • A protein shake or smoothie when solid food feels like too much
  • Oatmeal with a scoop of protein stirred in
  • Broth-based soups with some protein in them
  • Steamed or roasted soft vegetables
  • Soft fruit like banana, melon, and berries

The theme is simple, mild, and not greasy. Smoothies and soups are your friends on the days when chewing a full meal feels like a lot, because they get protein in without much effort.

What to go easy on

A handful of foods reliably make the first week harder. You do not need to ban them forever, just step back from them while your body adjusts:

  • Fried and greasy foods, which sit heavy and tend to trigger nausea
  • Very rich, creamy, or heavy dishes
  • Spicy food, if your stomach is already touchy
  • Sugary foods and drinks, which can spike queasiness
  • Large portions of anything, even healthy food
  • Alcohol, which hits differently on a much smaller appetite and can worsen side effects

If a food makes you feel worse, your body is telling you something useful. Listen to it this week rather than pushing through.

How to eat (the habits that prevent nausea)

How you eat matters as much as what you eat in week one:

  • Eat small and eat slowly. Put the fork down between bites and give your stomach time to register fullness.
  • Stop before you feel full, not after. Fullness arrives late and lands hard on a GLP-1.
  • Spread food across the day in small meals rather than two or three big ones.
  • Keep fluids separate from food. Drinking a lot during a meal fills you up fast and can push out the protein you need. Sip between meals instead.
  • Stay hydrated. Many people aim for roughly 2 to 3 liters of water a day unless their doctor advises otherwise, which also helps with the side effects below.

Managing the common side effects

Nausea is the most common visitor. Smaller portions, blander food, ginger or peppermint tea, fresh air, and not lying down right after eating all help. It usually fades as the week goes on.

Feeling full fast is not a side effect to fix, it is the medication working. Just make sure the few bites you take are the protein ones.

Constipation is common because you are eating and drinking less. Water, fiber from vegetables and soft fruit, and a little movement like a daily walk go a long way.

Fatigue can show up simply because you are eating less overall. This is another reason to make sure you are actually getting enough protein and not skipping meals entirely just because you are not hungry.

What a comfortable day can look like

This is an example, not a rule. Adjust everything to your appetite, and remember that eating a little of something is better than eating nothing because nothing sounded good.

  • Morning: scrambled eggs, or Greek yogurt with a few berries
  • Midday: a small portion of grilled chicken with soft, cooked vegetables
  • Afternoon: a protein shake or a handful of cottage cheese if real food feels like too much
  • Evening: white fish or a broth-based soup with some protein in it
  • Through the day: water, sipped steadily between meals

If even that feels like too much on a rough day, lean on smoothies and soups to keep protein coming in.

A little prep goes a long way

Decision-making is harder when you are queasy and not hungry, so the worst time to figure out what to eat is the moment you finally need to. Set yourself up before week one starts:

  • Cook a batch of plain chicken or fish you can grab from the fridge
  • Keep Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, eggs, and protein powder stocked
  • Have broth and a couple of easy soups ready
  • If cooking feels like too much during the adjustment, a meal delivery or prepped meals can take the decision off your plate entirely

The goal is that on day three, when nothing sounds good, the easy choice is already waiting for you.

When to call your doctor

Most first-week side effects are mild and pass. Reach out to your prescriber if you run into any of these:

  • Vomiting or diarrhea that will not stop, or that keeps you from holding fluids down
  • Signs of dehydration like dizziness, very dark urine, or a dry mouth that water is not fixing
  • Severe or persistent stomach pain, especially pain that spreads to your back
  • Any reaction that feels severe or scares you

You know your body. If something feels off rather than just uncomfortable, make the call. That is what your care team is there for.

The bottom line

Week one is temporary and predictable. Eat small, eat slowly, put protein first, sip water through the day, and go easy on the heavy and greasy stuff while your body settles. Do that and you get the part everyone actually wants: fat coming off while your muscle, your strength, and your metabolism stay right where they belong.

This article is for general information and is not medical advice. GLP-1 medications are prescription drugs, and your eating plan should be guided by the doctor or care team who prescribed yours. Always follow their guidance for your specific situation.
