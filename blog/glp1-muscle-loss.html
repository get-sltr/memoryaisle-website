39% of GLP-1 weight loss can be muscle.

The number that surprised researchers, and the small changes that protect your body composition without fighting your appetite.

8 min read · Body composition

When the first GLP-1 trials reported their weight loss numbers, the headline was the total. Fifteen percent. Twenty percent. Sometimes more. The number underneath got far less attention. In the STEP trial of semaglutide, around 39 percent of every pound lost wasn’t fat. It was lean mass — muscle, organs, water, and the connective tissue your body uses to do everything from climb stairs to regulate metabolism.

If you’re on a GLP-1 right now, that statistic is not a reason to stop. The medication is doing what it was designed to do, and the fat loss is real and meaningful. But the lean mass loss has a downstream cost most people don’t see coming. It’s worth talking about clearly.

Why this happens

GLP-1 receptor agonists work, in part, by quieting hunger signals. That’s the point. The problem is that quieting hunger doesn’t quiet your body’s protein needs. Your muscle is constantly being broken down and rebuilt, and rebuilding it requires amino acids you have to eat. When appetite drops 30 to 50 percent and protein drops with it, your body doesn’t politely wait for the situation to improve. It pulls amino acids from the largest protein store available — your skeletal muscle — and burns through it for energy and repair.

This isn’t a flaw in the medication. It’s basic physiology that applies to anyone who undereats protein during weight loss, whether the cause is a GLP-1, a crash diet, or a stomach flu that lasts three months. The GLP-1 just makes the undereating very, very easy to do without noticing.

Why it matters more than the number sounds

Losing lean mass isn’t only an aesthetic concern. Lean mass is metabolically active tissue. It’s what determines how many calories you burn at rest, how strong you feel walking up a flight of stairs in a year, and how easy it is to keep weight off when the medication eventually changes or stops.

There is also a regain question. Studies of weight loss followed by weight regain consistently show that what comes back is mostly fat, not the muscle that was lost. So a person who loses 30 pounds with high lean mass loss, then regains 15, ends up at a worse body composition than they started — even at the same scale weight.

A scale that shows steady weight loss can hide a body composition that is moving the wrong way.

The good news is that this outcome is largely avoidable, and the changes that prevent it are small. They are not “eat more” advice given to someone whose appetite is suppressed by design. They are precise, low-friction adjustments that work within the medication, not against it.

Three things that actually move the needle

I · Hit a daily protein floor, calculated from your lean mass

This is the single highest-leverage change. Protein intake during weight loss should be set as a floor you don’t fall below, not a goal you aim toward and miss. The right floor depends on your lean body mass, your activity level, and the phase of your medication cycle if you’re on an injectable. Generic advice like “30 grams a meal” is fine for some people and dramatically wrong for others. The number that protects your muscle is specific to you.

II · Train against resistance, even on the days you don’t feel like it

You don’t need a gym. You don’t need to lift heavy. What you need is a consistent signal to your body that the muscle is being used and should be retained. Two to three short sessions a week is enough to substantially reduce lean mass loss in published studies. The hardest day to train is often the day or two after an injection, when nausea peaks. Those are the days when a fifteen minute session matters most.

III · Track body composition, not just scale weight

A scale that shows steady weight loss can hide a body composition that is moving in the wrong direction. Most home scales now estimate body fat and lean mass with reasonable accuracy. The trend over weeks matters more than any single reading. If your scale weight is dropping but your lean mass is dropping at the same rate, you’re losing muscle. If your scale weight is dropping and your lean mass is stable or climbing, you’re doing it right.
